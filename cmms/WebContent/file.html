1. BACKGROUND

As announced back in November 2012 in the Mauritian Budget Speech 2013, the government is aiming to increase thecontrol and management requirements that GBC 1 companies will need to fulfill in order to benefit from DTAAs.After discussing with relevant stakeholders in that respect, the FSC has brought amendments to Section 3 of Chapter 4 of the Guide to Global Business on 4 September 2013. The objective of this measure is to bring the control and management requirements in Mauritius in line with OECD’s expectations andfurther improve the image of Mauritius as a clean and well-regulated international financial centre..

GBC 1 companies will have till 1 January 2015 to implement these new control and management requirements.

3. IMPLICATIONS

3.1      Treaty benefits     

As from 1 January 2015, all GBC 1s wishing to avail treaty benefits will have to demonstrate additionalcontrol and management in Mauritius apart from the existing ones (that is Section 3.2 of Chapter 4 of the FSC Guide). The GBC 1 will however have a choice on how to demonstrate the additional control and management requirements (that is the new Section 3.3 of the amended Chapter 4 of the FSC Guide). A GBC 1 can thus meet at least one of the following additional criteria:

·         Have an office in Mauritius;

·         Employ at least 1 administrative/technical staff in Mauritius;

·         Having an arbitration clause in its Constitution whereby all disputes arising out of the constitution shall be resolved by way of arbitration in Mauritius[1].;

·         Hold assets (excluding cash and shares/interests) of at least USD 100,000;

·         List its shares on a securities exchange in Mauritius; or

·         Incur expenditure in Mauritius as might be expected of a similar corporation in Mauritius.

Therefore, a GBC 1 may not obtain a Tax Residence Certificate if the new control and management requirements are not met.

3.2      Collective Investment Schemes

Collective investment schemes, closed end funds and external pension schemes having a GBC 1 licence should be administered from Mauritius and demonstrate the enhanced control and management as per Section 3.3 of Chapter 4. Currently, most of the collective investment schemes are administered from Mauritius, and therefore this new requirement will not be onerous on existing collective investment schemes.

However,  the other requirements mentioned in 3.2 above (that is, office in Mauritius, employing staff, arbitration clause, holding of assets, listing or incurring expenditure) would also apply to collective investment schemes holding a GBC 1 licence. These new requirements may have an impact on the NAV of some collective investment schemes but there is the possibility of choosing the less costly option among any one of the new control and management requirements. 

3.3      Related Corporation

A GBC 1 would be deemed to comply with the new control and management requirements   if a related corporationalready complies with same. A related corporation refers to a subsidiary, fellow subsidiary[2], a parent corporation or any other corporation within the same group.

3.4      FSC Circular Letter – Additional Requirements for resident directors

The FSC has also imposed additional requirements on resident directors through the FSC Circular Letter dated 28 March 2013. The Circular Letter provides that resident directors of licensees of the FSC (including GBC 1s and CIS) should:

·         Have relevant qualification and experience to exercise sufficient care, diligence and skills for the good conduct of the business;

·         Act with integrity and freedom of mind, without any influence, interest of relationship that might impair his professional judgment or objectivity;

·         Provide impartial and good judgement in his duties; and

·         Ensure that sufficient time and attention is given to affairs of each board on which the director sits by adequately preparing for each meeting, attending all meetings, actively participate in board deliberations etc.

The FSC will consider all the above factors upon the receipt of application for a new licence, on the renewal of an existing licence or on approval/ notification ofappointments of new directors.

The FSC has not defined what would amount to relevant qualification or experience. It seems that the FSC will consider the previous professional experience of a person and his/her academic or other qualifications, and thereby determine whether that person can fulfill his/her duties as director of a company.

The global business industry has reacted positively to the above FSC Circular Letter as these new requirements on resident directors were issued following consultation with the industry.

4. COMMENTS

The enhanced control and management requirements may seem daunting for some GBC 1s. However these requirements are not completely new as some existing GBC 1s are already complying with some of them.The ability to choose among the different ways to demonstrate control and managementalso means that GBC 1s can go for the option best suited to them

It would seem from face value, that providing in the constitution for the seat of arbitration to be in Mauritius is the less costly and time-consuming option, assuming that the shareholders of a GBC 1 consider arbitration as their option for resolving disputes arising out of the constitution.

On the other hand, employing staff, incurring expenditure, holding assets or listing on securities exchangesin Mauritius will help to consolidate and reaffirm a GBC 1’s physical presence in Mauritius.These would therefore seem to be more credible options vis àvis tax authorities and are suitablefor companies aiming for tax certainty.

Model of Arbitration Clause

“Any dispute arising out of a breach of this Constitution is to be settled amicably between the Shareholders. In the event the Shareholders fail to resolve a dispute within a period of three (3) months of the date on which the dispute has arisen, the dispute shall be resolved by a single arbitrator. The arbitration shall take place in Mauritius and the language of the arbitration’s proceeding shall be English. The arbitrator shall be a barrister entitled to practice law in Mauritius and shall be appointed by the mutual agreement of the Shareholders. On failure by the Shareholders to agree on the name of the arbitrator, any Shareholder may make an application to a Judge sitting in chambers for the appointment of such arbitrator. The award given by the arbitrator shall be conclusive, final and binding on all Shareholders and shall not be subject to appeal.”
